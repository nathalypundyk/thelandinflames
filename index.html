AMAZON RAINFOREST

I am the jaguar, guardian of the Amazon rainforest. This place, once teeming with life, is now a sea of ashes. The Amazon, known as the lungs of the world, has seen millions of hectares burn in recent years. The fires of the last 20 years have consumed over 120 million hectares almost the size of Peru. These fires have released large amounts of CO₂, worsening the climate change that already affects us. It is estimated that in 2019 alone, the fires in the Amazon released more than 220 megatons of carbon dioxide about 47 million cars driven per year.

BOLIVIA

In Bolivia, I am one of the thousands who have seen their homes disappear in flames. The fire does not discriminate; it consumes both trees and animals. The year 2019 was particularly devastating: more than 5 million hectares were destroyed, and it is estimated that hundreds of species disappeared or saw their populations reduced.  These fires are often caused by burning land for agriculture, but climate change makes everything worse. Prolonged droughts dry out our soils, leaving plants ready to burn.

CHILE

I am the condor, and from above, I have witnessed how fires devour the forests in Chile. In 2017 alone, more than 500,000 hectares were destroyed by fire.  Stronger winds and higher temperatures, caused by climate change, make these fires uncontrollable. In recent years, we have seen how fire spreads faster and with more ferocity. In February 2024, 137 people died after the fire spread from the forest to the city.

ARGENTINA

In Argentina, forest fires have also devastated large areas of the Chaco region and the Misiones rainforest. We are part of a rich biodiversity, but fire has no mercy. In 2020, more than 300,000 hectares were destroyed, wiping out the habitats of countless species.  Forest fires, which used to be part of Earth's natural cycle, are now becoming much more intense and difficult to control.

PARAGUAY

In Paraguay, I have seen the fire consume the dry forests of the Chaco. In 2020, fires devoured more than 350,000 hectares of vegetation. Fires not only destroy our homes but the smoke covers the sky, making life difficult for both animals and humans. Fire seasons are now longer, as extreme heat extends throughout the year.

ECUADOR

In Ecuador, forest fires have begun to threaten the páramos and Andean forests, which are crucial to the ecosystem.  The páramos, which are essential for water capture, are in danger due to these fires. Quito declared an emergency since in recent years it has experienced catastrophic fires.

URUGUAY

I am the capybara, a resident of Uruguay's wetlands. Although fires are not as frequent here as in other countries, climate change has increased the risk. In 2021, several fires affected protected areas, destroying the habitat of many species like mine.  The loss of biodiversity and the destruction of wetlands affect the entire food chain.

VENEZUELA

In Venezuela, forest fires have ravaged the Andean lands, where I and other animals live. In 2018, thousands of hectares burned, affecting many of my companions.  These fires not only destroy the tropical forest but also affect the indigenous and rural communities that depend on the forests for their livelihoods.

PERU

In Peru, I am the spectacled bear, known for inhabiting the Andes and mountainous regions. Although forest fires are not as frequent here as in other countries in the Amazon region, we also face significant threats. In recent years, we have seen an increase in the frequency of fires due to climate change and human practices such as agriculture and illegal logging. Between July and September 2024, at least 20 people died in 361 fires recorded in 22 regions of Peru.

COLOMBIA

In Colombia, I am the pink dolphin, a resident of the rivers and wetlands of the Amazon and Orinoco. Although we do not usually live in areas directly affected by forest fires, the fire and deforestation severely affect our aquatic habitats. In 2020, Colombia lost more than 160,000 hectares of the Amazon rainforest due to fires and indiscriminate logging. Furthermore, fires increase sedimentation in rivers and affect water quality, putting our survival at risk.
